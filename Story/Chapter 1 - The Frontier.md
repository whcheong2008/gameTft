# Chapter 1 — The Frontier

The settlement sat at the edge of the world, or close enough to it. Beyond the wooden palisade, the grasslands stretched east until they blurred into something wrong — a shimmer where the horizon met the sky, like heat haze that never lifted. The locals called it the Veil's breath. Nobody went that far.

Kael studied the settlement from the ridge, counting the gaps in the palisade where logs had rotted or been torn out. Three obvious breaches on the north side. Two on the east. The watchtower on the southeast corner was listing.

"They're not going to like us," Lyric said, settling beside him. He was eating an apple he'd conjured from somewhere — he always had food.

"We're not here to be liked."

"We're also not here to be feared. These people have been fighting Voidspawn for months without support. If we walk in like military inspectors, they'll smile to our faces and ignore everything we say." He took another bite. "Let me handle the introductions."

Behind them, Senna was performing a final attunement check on their Echo bonds. She sat cross-legged on the grass, eyes closed, fingers twitching as if plucking invisible strings. Each Resonant carried a bond to an Echo — an elemental spirit pulled through the Veil from the Otherside — and each bond required a Veilborn like Senna to maintain it. Without her, the Echoes would drift, weaken, and eventually snap back through the barrier. She was the reason they could fight.

"Kael, your Earth Echo is pulling left," she said without opening her eyes. "Are you stressed?"

"No."

"Liar." Her fingers adjusted something invisible. "Lyric, yours is humming. Good mood?"

"Always."

"I know both of you better than you know yourselves." She opened her eyes. "Don't forget that."

Otho — the fourth member of their group, an elderly Veilborn scholar who'd requested to tag along "to study frontier Veil patterns" — was crouched nearby, scraping soil into a glass vial. He muttered about residual densities and harmonic frequencies. He'd walked into a tree twice on the journey here.

They descended into the settlement.

---

Lyric handled the introductions. He shook hands with the militia captain, learned her name (Torren), learned her children's names (Kira and Sol), learned that the grain stores were low and the eastern breach was the worst. Within ten minutes, the settlement's stiffness had melted into something closer to relief. Help had arrived. Real help — Resonants.

Kael addressed the militia volunteers. Twelve nervous civilians with essence-forged spears and repurposed armor. The weapons were crude — Veil Essence hammered into steel by a frontier blacksmith — but they held an edge that could cut through Voidspawn hide where normal metal couldn't. The volunteers themselves were the weak link. Brave enough to stand on a wall, but a world apart from what a Resonant could do.

Kael laid out the formation: where to stand, how to hold the line, when to fall back. Precise. Efficient. The volunteers listened with the rigid attention of people who didn't quite understand what they were hearing.

Lyric stepped in. "What he means is — stay behind the big ones, don't wander off, and if something ugly charges you, let one of us deal with it. You're here to learn, not to die."

The volunteers laughed. The tension broke.

This was the pattern. Kael built the plan. Lyric made it human.

---

Senna gathered the volunteers for a different kind of briefing — explaining what Veilborn did and why the group needed her.

"Think of the Veil as a wall between us and the Otherside," she said, gesturing at the faint shimmer on the eastern horizon. "On one side is the Luminous Plane — that's where Echoes come from. Peaceful spirits. Helpful. When a Veilborn like me thins the Veil on that side, we can call them through and bind them to Resonants like Kael and Lyric."

A volunteer raised her hand. "And the Voidspawn?"

"Different world entirely. The Void — a corrupted dimension on the other side of the Veil. The Voidspawn come through their own cracks. We Veilborn can mend those tears when we find them." She smiled. "Two worlds, two doors. We work the good one."

The volunteers nodded. It made sense.

---

Their first engagements were straightforward. A Voidspawn patrol had been probing the northern breach — low-tier creatures, barely organized. Kael positioned the militia behind a screen of summoned Echoes and let the spirits do the heavy lifting. The militia's essence-forged weapons could wound Voidspawn, but killing them efficiently required an Echo's elemental force — and a Resonant to channel it.

Even before he called on his Echoes, Kael could feel the difference between himself and the militia. Resonance changed the body permanently — faster reflexes, denser muscle, a sharpness of perception that never fully switched off. He'd been like this since his first attunement at fifteen. It was as much a part of him as his eye color. The Echoes and the Veil connection were something else — layered on top, amplifying what was already there — but the baseline was his. It would be his whether the Veil existed or not.

When he channeled, the surge went further. Heightened reflexes became superhuman. The sense of being *more* sharpened into something almost painful.

"Hold formation," he called. "Let them come to you."

Lyric worked the militia's flank, keeping the volunteers steady. A young man named Dren was in his first real fight, and his hands were shaking so badly he could barely hold his spear. Lyric didn't call it out. He just shifted position, putting himself between Dren and the worst of the fighting, and when the Voidspawn broke, he clapped the boy on the shoulder.

"Nice work, Dren."

Dren hadn't done anything. But he stood a little taller.

After the fight, Kael was already reviewing the next objective. Lyric noticed Dren sitting apart, staring at his hands.

"First one's always the worst," Lyric said, sitting down next to him. "Gets easier."

Later, walking back: "Dren's going to be fine."

Kael looked at him. "You always know their names."

"You always know where they should stand. We cover different things."

---

The engagements grew harder as they pushed further from the settlement. The Voidspawn were more aggressive out here — better organized, hitting in coordinated waves. The militia held their line with essence-forged steel and grim determination, but the Echoes did the real work — elemental energy tearing through the corrupted creatures while Kael directed the battle from the front and Lyric held the human line together behind him.

At camp that night, Senna performed the daily attunement. It was routine — she did it while chatting, the way someone might mend clothes or brush hair. Kael noticed Otho in the background, hunched over his instruments with a frown he'd been wearing all day.

"Something wrong?" Kael asked.

"Hmm? Oh. No. Just... unusual readings. The Veil density here is higher than expected for a frontier zone. I assumed the settlement's Veilborn quarter would create a minor thinning, but the data suggests..." He trailed off, scribbling. "Never mind. Frontier anomaly."

---

A family on the settlement's eastern edge refused to evacuate despite the increasing Voidspawn pressure. Father, mother, two children. Their farm was everything they had.

"We should override them," Lyric said. "We know better. They'll die out here if a real surge hits."

"It's their home," Kael said. "Their choice."

But he didn't just leave it at that. He spent an hour with the father reinforcing the farmhouse's entry points, showed him where to barricade if something came through the eastern breach, and left them two spare essence-forged short spears from the militia stockpile. "Keep these by the door. Aim for the joints — that's where the hide is thinnest."

The father nodded. The mother watched from the doorway with one child on her hip and the other hiding behind her leg.

When the group passed on patrol the next day, the family waved from the yard. The children were playing near the well. The father had already mounted one of the spears by the front door.

---

After clearing a Voidspawn incursion near the Veilborn quarter, Otho examined the emergence sites with his instruments while Kael stood watch.

"The emergence pattern is interesting," Otho said, more to himself than to Kael. "The Void tears opened closest to the Veilborn quarter. The Voidspawn probably prioritize us because we're their biggest threat — we seal their tears, we empower their enemies. Of course they'd target us first."

He tapped his pen against his notebook.

"What I can't quite account for is the precision. They come from the Void. A different world from the Luminous Plane entirely. So how does a creature from over there know, immediately upon arrival, exactly where the Veilborn are standing over here? We're connected to the Luminous side, not theirs."

Senna, nearby, didn't look up from her attunement work. "They sense Veil energy. Everyone knows that."

"Yes." Otho wrote something in his notebook. "Everyone knows that."

---

The Veil Warden was waiting at the edge of the settlement's territory — a massive, corrupted guardian spirit that had once been benign. Something had twisted it. It stood three meters tall, elemental energy crackling across its stone-like skin, and when it moved, the ground trembled.

"It was benign once," Senna said as they approached. She could feel its Echo signature — damaged, corrupted, in pain. "Something twisted it."

"We put it down," Kael said. "Lyric, you handle morale. I'll call positions."

The fight was Kael's kind of fight — straightforward, tactical. The Warden was powerful but predictable. It telegraphed its attacks with a red-zone shimmer on the ground, giving them seconds to reposition. When it dropped to half health, it roared and surged — faster, stronger, angrier.

"Red zone!" Kael called. "Reposition away from the center!"

"Hold steady!" Lyric called to the militia, who were watching from behind the line. "We've got this!"

Senna strained at the attunement, keeping the Echo bonds stable as the Warden's corruption tried to interfere. "It's drawing on the Veil... I can feel it pulling."

The Warden fell. Stone crumbled. The corrupted energy dissipated. The militia erupted in a ragged cheer. Lyric made sure Dren was part of it — pulled the young man into the celebration, made him feel like he'd contributed.

Afterward, in the quiet, Senna said: "These corruptions are happening faster than they used to."

Otho, writing in his notebook: "Yes. They are."

Kael and Lyric shared a look across the fire — comfortable, familiar. The mission was going well. The group worked. The world made sense.

Lyric clapped Dren on the shoulder one final time. "You survived your first boss. Drinks are on me."

The settlement was safe. The frontier was held. They'd done what they came to do.

Kael didn't know, sitting there in the firelight with his best friend and his team, that this was the last time everything would feel simple.
