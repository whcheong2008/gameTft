# Chapter 3 — The Synergy Trials

Two more Resonants joined the group before they deployed east.

**Sera** arrived from the capital academy — a mage specialist. She assessed Kael in the first ten seconds, assessed Lyric in the next ten, and decided she respected Lyric more. Kael noticed.

**Maren** came from a field hospital in the southern provinces — a healer who'd spent two years mending Resonants after bad deployments. Within a day, she and Senna were trading attunement techniques like old friends. Within two days, Mira was sitting closer to Maren at meals than anyone except Senna.

---

The deployment was to a contested region in the east — multiple settlements under simultaneous Voidspawn assault, the attacks coordinated in a way that suggested something behind them. Intelligence. Strategy. The settlements couldn't defend themselves in isolation. They needed to work together. That was the mission: synergy. Make the pieces cooperate.

The early engagements were clean. Kael paired the group in tactical combinations — Ren's immovable defense with Sera's devastating burst, Maren's healing sustain with Lyric's aggressive pressure. Each pairing worked because the archetypes complemented each other. Guardians held the line while Predators flanked. Rangers kept distance while Sages kept everyone alive.

Sera was as good as advertised. Her Echo-channeled magic hit like a siege weapon — precise, overwhelming. She didn't need direction. She read the field as fast as Kael did and acted on it without waiting for orders.

In the third engagement, a coordinated assault against a Voidspawn nest on the settlement's edge, Sera saw an opening and took it. The enemy formation was thin at the center, and if she could break through there, the creatures scattered into two fighting groups could be isolated and destroyed separately. She overchanneled her Echo magic, pulling ambient Veil energy from the contested territory itself and weaving it into her burst casting. Power threaded through her like lightning — more than she'd ever channeled before, more than any controlled attunement should have allowed.

The devastation was instantaneous. The enemy line didn't just break — it *detonated*. Creatures scattered screaming. The engagement ended in seconds.

Afterward, Kael found her sitting apart from the group, breathing hard, her Echo bond visible as a stuttering flicker of light around her wrist. It shouldn't be flickering. A stable bond was the foundation of everything a Resonant did.

"Your bond just destabilized for four seconds," Kael said.

Sera didn't look at him. "We won faster."

"And if it had snapped entirely?"

"Then I would have fixed it." She finally met his eyes. "Bonds snap. Healers mend them. That's the job."

Kael left her there, but Lyric, watching from across camp, was already grinning. Kael understood his expression perfectly: *This is why I like her.*

"She's going to be a problem," Lyric said that evening, amused.

"She's effective," Kael said.

"Both things."

---

The third settlement they were sent to defend was called Hallen. It sat on a hill, surrounded by farmland, with a Veilborn quarter on the western slope. Twenty-three Veilborn lived there — attunists, scholars, a few children. They maintained the local Echo bonds and mended Veil tears when they found them. The settlement's defensive line depended on their position as an anchor point.

A Voidspawn advance was bearing down from the north. Hundreds of creatures, organized, pushing a front line across the farmland. If it hit the settlement, Hallen would hold — the defenses were solid, the militia trained. But the cost would be high. Twenty, maybe thirty dead.

Kael studied the maps. There were two options.

Evacuate the village: save the people, but lose the Veil-anchor position, which would destabilize the entire region's defensive network. More attacks would get through in the weeks ahead. More settlements would fall.

Hold the village: keep the anchor, keep the network intact. But Hallen's militia would take the brunt. Twenty to thirty dead — maybe more, depending on how hard the Voidspawn pressed.

Lyric saw the math immediately. "We hold it. Twenty die here so two hundred don't die across the region over the next month."

Kael looked at the settlement below them. People in the streets. Children playing near the well. A woman hanging laundry. "I won't order them to stand and die in their own homes."

"They're militia. They signed up for this."

"They signed up to defend their families. Not to be a calculation."

Maren, who had been listening quietly, said: "There's a middle option. Partial evacuation — get the non-combatants out, keep the militia and the Veilborn in place. Split the defense between us and the locals."

They compromised. It worked, but messily. The defense held, the anchor was preserved, but the split deployment meant thinner lines everywhere. The Voidspawn found the gaps. Twelve soldiers died in positions that a full defense might have covered. Maybe all of them. Maybe none. There was no way to know.

Lyric didn't say anything about it. Not that night, not the next morning. But Kael saw it — Lyric smiled at the militia's debrief and the smile didn't reach his eyes.

---

That night, the group camped on the ridge above Hallen. The fires from the clean-up below cast orange light on the clouds.

Lyric came to Kael's fire late. He sat for a long time before speaking.

"The compromise cost more lives than holding would have."

"It saved the people in the village."

"Twelve extra soldiers died for that."

"We don't trade lives, Lyric."

Lyric stared into the fire. "Everyone trades lives, Kael. Some people just don't count."

Silence. The fire crackled. Neither was angry. They were both scared — scared of where this conversation went if they followed it to its end. So they stopped.

Later that night, after the fires had burned down and most of the camp was asleep, Maren found Lyric sitting alone at the edge of the ridge. The silence between them wasn't uncomfortable. She sat beside him without being asked.

"You know the worst part isn't being wrong," she said quietly. "It's being right and watching it not matter."

Lyric looked at her. Surprise flickered across his face — someone had seen into the thing he was carrying.

"I don't think you're right," Maren continued. "But I see what it costs you to be willing to be."

She didn't wait for a response. She just stood and walked back toward the camp, leaving Lyric with his thoughts and the fire's dying light.

---

The next morning, Senna pulled Kael aside before the daily attunement.

"I'm going to work with you and Lyric separately for a while," she said. "Your bonds are interfering with each other. When you're tense, it pulls on his resonance, and when he withdraws, it makes yours tighter. I can smooth it out better one at a time."

"We're fine, Senna."

"I know you're fine. I also know what your bonds feel like, and right now they sound like two instruments trying to play different songs. Let me tune you properly." She gave him the look that meant the conversation was over. "It's just attunement. Don't read into it."

---

Senna had been teaching Mira attunement between engagements — basic exercises, how to read a bond's stability, how to feel the Veil's proximity. During one session, an Echo crystal Mira was practicing on flickered — the dormant spirit inside it twitching toward something Mira couldn't identify.

"It moved," Mira said. "I wasn't doing anything."

"They do that sometimes," Senna said, adjusting the crystal. "Echoes drift. Especially this close to contested territory — there's more Veil activity, more ambient noise. They hear something in it and lean toward it. Part of the job is pulling them back."

"What are they hearing?"

Senna considered the question with the seriousness she gave all of Mira's questions. "Honestly? I don't know. I've felt it for years — a kind of pull in the background that the Echoes respond to. It's probably just Veil static. Noise from the other side."

"Probably?"

"Probably." Senna smiled. "Now — try the exercise again. Keep the bond steady this time."

Mira tried again. The crystal stayed still.

---

The engagements continued across the region — settlement after settlement, coordination after coordination. The group moved fast, fought clean, and left each position stronger than they found it. Kael directed. Lyric inspired. Ren held. Sera destroyed. Maren healed. The machine worked.

But Kael and Lyric no longer sat at the same fire.

---

At the fourth settlement — a Veilborn sanctuary guarded by a military garrison — Kael drew watch duty alongside a veteran soldier. The man was older, weathered, with the patient stillness of someone who'd been standing on walls for decades. His name was Harl. He'd been stationed at this sanctuary for twenty years.

They stood in silence for a while. Harl didn't seem to need conversation. Kael appreciated that.

"Twenty years on this post," Harl said eventually. "You start to notice things. Patterns. Stuff Command doesn't put in the reports because it's not actionable."

Kael glanced at him.

"The attacks track the headcount," Harl said. "When the sanctuary is full — thirty, forty Veilborn in residence — we get hit hard. Void tears opening along the perimeter every other day, sometimes every day. When most of them deploy on missions and we're down to five or six, the attacks almost stop. Stragglers. Probes. Nothing serious."

"Makes sense," Kael said. "More Veilborn means a stronger Veil energy concentration. Bigger signal."

Harl nodded slowly. "That's what I figured too. For about the first ten years." He was quiet for a moment. "But here's the thing. When they come back from missions, the attacks resume within *hours*. Not the next day. That night. Before dawn. Every time. If the Voidspawn are detecting a stronger signal and then mustering forces and then marching here, that takes time. Days, at least. But the response is almost instant."

"Maybe they're always nearby. The signal activates them."

"Maybe." Harl picked at a callus on his palm. "Let me tell you one more thing and then I'll shut up. About six years ago, a group of twelve Veilborn researchers arrived in the middle of the night. Unannounced. They came through the eastern pass on foot, no convoy, no signal fires — security measure. Nobody outside these walls knew they were coming. Even I didn't know until they were knocking on the gate."

He paused.

"By dawn, there were three new Void tears along the eastern perimeter. The biggest surge we'd had in months. Whatever was out there didn't need to see a convoy or detect a signal fire. Twelve Veilborn arrived in secret, and within hours the attacks adjusted."

He looked at Kael. Leaned against his spear.

"Sometimes it feels less like they're detecting us and more like they're... arriving with us. Like we bring them in our luggage."

He laughed at his own metaphor — a short, dry sound.

Kael smiled politely. "That's an interesting way to put it."

"Ignore me. Too many night watches."

They finished the shift in silence.

---

On their last day in the region, after clearing the approach to the Twin Heralds' lair, the group crested a ridge and stopped.

The sky to the east was wrong.

Not dark — *dense*. Like a bruise on the horizon, something vast and solid where open sky should be. It didn't move like clouds. It didn't shimmer like the Veil's breath. It just sat there, massive and still, as if the sky itself had developed a tumor.

The ground hummed. Not vibrated — hummed. A low, subsonic frequency that Kael felt in his teeth.

Otho stared at it. His hand went to his notebook, hovered over the page, and dropped. He didn't write. For the first time since Kael had known him, Otho looked at something interesting and didn't record it.

Senna put a hand on his shoulder. "What is it?"

"I hope I'm wrong," Otho said.

He walked away. He didn't explain.

---

The Twin Heralds were the region's final challenge — two massive Voidspawn that fought in tandem, a melee brute and an arcane caster. When they stood close together, they amplified each other's power. When one died, the survivor went berserk.

Kael split the team without discussing it with Lyric. Lyric split his half the same way. They didn't need to coordinate. The instinct was identical — separate the Heralds, whittle both to critical, then burst simultaneously. Clean. Surgical. The kind of tactic they'd developed together over years of fighting side by side.

Ren anchored the melee Herald, absorbing its charges like a boulder in a river. Sera poured magic into the caster Herald, forcing it back. Maren kept both teams alive from the center, her healing stretched across the battlefield. Kael called the timing: "Both at critical. On my mark — *now.*"

Both Heralds fell within three seconds of each other. No enrage. No berserk phase. Perfect execution.

Afterward, they sat apart. Kael at one fire, reviewing maps. Lyric at another, talking with the militia. The physical distance was only a few meters.

Otho sat in his tent with his notebook open, cross-referencing the Barracks archive data with observations he'd been collecting since the Frontier. He worked late. He didn't sleep well.

The group packed up the next morning and moved east, toward the Shattered Lands. Toward the thing on the horizon that Otho hoped he was wrong about.

He wasn't.
