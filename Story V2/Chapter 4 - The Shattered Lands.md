# Chapter 4 — The Shattered Lands

The world changed at the border.

One step: solid earth, brown grass, the familiar weight of a sky that behaved itself. The next step: the ground was translucent. The grass was crystal. The sky flickered — mortal blue, then alien violet, then blue again — like a lamp with a loose connection. And through the flickering, glimpses of something vast on the other side. Not the Luminous Plane or the Void, as they'd been taught. Just... the Otherside. Undifferentiated. Immense.

Wild Echoes drifted through the air like luminous jellyfish, unbound and purposeless. They responded to nothing — not Senna's attunement, not the group's presence. They just floated, half-real, flickering in and out of existence. Alongside them, Voidspawn prowled openly. Neither seemed to notice the other. They coexisted in this place where the Veil was tissue-thin, both equally at home and equally alien.

Senna stopped at the border. Her face was tight. "The attunement bonds feel noisy here. Like trying to tune an instrument in a windstorm."

The eastern horizon was no longer a bruise. It was a wall. A slow-moving mass of corruption that reshaped everything beneath it — land, air, light. The Sovereign. Not a creature. A geography. A fact of nature that happened to be advancing.

Otho stared at it with his notebook open and his pen still. He hadn't written anything since the ridge.

---

They'd been sent here because the Shattered Lands were getting worse.

Military reports had been trickling in for weeks — the Veil instability in this region was escalating. Tears that normally opened and closed in hours were staying open for days. New ones were forming faster than Veilborn teams could mend them. The Voidspawn emerging from them were stronger, more organized, and pressing outward in all directions. The Shattered Lands had always been a problem zone, but now they were becoming an eruption point — a wound in the Veil that was spreading.

The group's mission was reconnaissance: assess the situation, determine if the eruption could be contained, and report back. Simple enough on paper. But as they pushed deeper into the territory, fighting through Voidspawn that materialized from tears opening mid-step, Kael began to understand that "containment" was optimistic. The tears were opening everywhere. Not just where the Veil was thinnest — everywhere. The terrain shifted under their feet. The sky couldn't decide which world it belonged to.

And it was getting worse by the day.

---

The observatory was a ruin of ancient stone and corroded instruments — astronomical equipment that had once measured the Veil itself, now covered in crystalline growths. Otho went inside with the focus of a man who'd been searching for something specific.

"I need time with these records," he said. "Keep the perimeter clear."

The group defended the observatory while Otho worked. Voidspawn hit in waves — the assaults were heavier here, more frequent, as if the eruption was accelerating around them.

When Otho emerged, his hands were shaking. He carried ancient charts — star maps, population records, Veil measurements.

"I need to verify something. Give me tonight."

"What did you find?" Kael asked.

"Give me tonight."

---

"We need to see the Sovereign up close," Kael said the next morning. "The eruption is centered on it. If we're going to report anything useful, we need to understand what's driving it."

They didn't get to just observe.

The Sovereign's perimeter wasn't a line — it was a gradient. The terrain grew more alien with every step. Crystal replaced trees. The air tasted like ozone. And the Voidspawn here were different. Stronger. They emerged from tears that opened and closed in a rhythm, like breathing.

The group engaged. Kael called positions. Echoes clashed with Voidspawn in the corrupted landscape. But something was wrong. The Voidspawn they killed were replaced almost immediately — new tears opening, new creatures emerging. And the Sovereign itself, visible as a massive presence looming over the field, pulsed.

It *pulsed*. Every time Senna strained harder to maintain the attunement bonds — every time the Resonants pushed their Echoes harder — the Sovereign brightened. The tears opened faster. The replacements came quicker.

Sixty seconds. That was all they lasted before Kael called the retreat. Sixty seconds of combat that accomplished nothing — every Voidspawn they killed was replaced, and the Sovereign seemed stronger for the effort.

They pulled back in silence.

---

Lyric was the first to speak. His voice was quiet. Flat.

"Did you see it? When Senna pushed harder, it got stronger. We were feeding it."

Kael had no answer.

---

That night, Otho presented his findings.

He gathered everyone in the observatory's main chamber — around a stone table covered in ancient charts and his own notebook pages. He'd been cross-referencing the data he'd copied at the Barracks archive with the observatory's pre-Shattering records. Birth registries of Veilborn. Veil-crossing event logs. Population maps.

He spoke slowly. Carefully.

"The ancient scholars tracked every crossing event for generations," he began. "Echoes and Voidspawn. They didn't separate them — they used one word for both. *Veilcrossers*. We assumed that was because they hadn't discovered the two planes yet. The Luminous Plane and the Void."

He laid out a chart. Columns of numbers, lines of correlation.

"They didn't separate them because there was nothing to separate."

Silence. Mira looked between the adults, reading their faces.

"The birth registries map Veilborn population by region and year. The crossing logs map *all* Veil events — Echoes and Voidspawn — by the same regions and years. The correlation is absolute."

He pointed at the chart. His finger was trembling.

"When the last Veilborn in a region died, the tears sealed and *everything* stopped. Echoes and Voidspawn both. When new Veilborn were born, *both* returned. Every surge in recorded history maps exactly to Veilborn population growth. Every lull maps to population decline."

His voice broke once. On the word "origin."

"There is no Luminous Plane. There is no Void. There is one Otherside, and the Veilborn are the only opening. Echoes and Voidspawn are the same kind of entity — *Veilcrossers* — coming through the same door. The corruption that distinguishes Voidspawn is simply what happens when crossing energy accumulates without guidance. Like water: harmless in a stream. Destructive in a flood."

Senna went white. Not gradually — all at once, as if someone had drained the color from her face.

Mira looked at Senna. Then at Otho. Then at her own hands.

"What does that mean?" she asked. Her voice was very small. "That we cause them?"

Nobody answered her.

"The Sovereign," Otho continued, "isn't an ancient evil from the Void pressing inward. It's a manifestation — an accumulation of all the corruption that has leaked through the Veil over centuries of Veilborn existence. It grows as long as any Veilborn live. And the eruption here — the tears spreading outward, getting worse every day — this is what happens when the accumulation reaches a tipping point. The Veil is breaking down. Not locally. Globally. The Shattered Lands are where the break started, but it's spreading. Every day we don't close that door, the break gets wider."

"Close the Veil," Lyric said. Not a question. A conclusion.

"Kill every Veilborn," Otho said, "and the Veil seals permanently. No more Echoes. No more Voidspawn. The Sovereign, cut off from its source..."

"Dies," Lyric finished.

---

The silence after the revelation lasted hours.

The group made camp in the observatory ruins. Nobody spoke much. Senna sat with Mira, who had finally, fully understood and wouldn't let go of Senna's hand. Otho was hunched over his notes, rechecking calculations he'd already checked. Ren stood watch. Sera sat alone. Maren sat between the Veilborn and the Resonants, belonging to neither side.

Lyric sat alone, staring at nothing.

And outside the observatory walls, the tears kept spreading. They could hear new ones opening in the distance — a sound like fabric ripping, over and over, getting closer. Every hour they spent deliberating was an hour the eruption grew.

---

He came to Kael at midnight.

The fire had burned low. The observatory walls flickered between stone and crystal. Outside, another tear opened somewhere close — the ripping sound followed by the wet hiss of something crossing through.

Lyric's voice wasn't angry. It was exhausted. Gentle, almost.

"You were there. You saw it. Every time Senna pushed harder, it got stronger. The data says why."

Kael didn't respond.

"There are maybe four thousand Veilborn in the world. The Sovereign has absorbed at least thirty settlements. And the eruption here is accelerating — the tears are spreading outward, Kael. Not just here. Outward. If we don't close the Veil, this becomes everywhere. Every city. Every village. The entire world, Shattered."

"I know."

"Then you know what has to happen."

Silence.

"I'm not saying I want this." Lyric's voice cracked. There were tears on his face. "I'm saying there's no other answer. Four thousand lives against — what? Millions? Everyone? This isn't a difficult calculation. It's the easiest calculation in the world. It's just the hardest thing I've ever had to say."

Kael's refusal came from somewhere words couldn't reach.

"You're asking me to kill Senna."

"I know."

"You're asking me to kill Mira. She's sixteen."

"I know."

"You're asking me to murder every person who has ever trusted us to protect them."

"I'm asking you to save everyone else."

The fire crackled. Outside, another tear ripped open. Something crossed through. Something else killed it. The sounds of a world coming apart at the seams, one tear at a time.

"My father could have saved our village," Kael said. "All he had to do was hand over the refugees. Seventeen people for a whole village. Easy math."

"Your father's village burned, Kael."

"And the seventeen survived. And they built a new village. And their children are alive today."

"And your father is dead. And so is everyone who lived next to him."

"I won't do it," Kael said. "And I won't let you do it."

"Then people are going to die." Lyric wiped his face with the back of his hand. "A lot of people. Because you couldn't."

He stood and walked away. The fire died to embers. Another tear opened in the distance.

---

The next morning, Lyric walked toward the tent where the Veilborn slept.

He walked openly. In daylight. He didn't sneak, didn't creep, didn't try to be subtle. He believed he was right, and he didn't think he should have to hide it. His hand was on his weapon.

Kael stepped into his path.

Everything had already been said. Midnight had used up all the words. What was left was simpler and worse.

They fought.

Not metaphorically — Echoes clashing, energy rippling across the corrupted landscape. Lyric fought the way he always fought: smart, aggressive, adaptive. He used the tactics they'd developed together. He knew where Kael positioned, knew his tendencies, knew his openings. Fighting Lyric was like fighting a mirror.

But Lyric wasn't trying to kill Kael. He was trying to get *past* him — pathing around the defense, angling toward the tent where Senna and Mira and Otho waited. Kael saw it in every feint, every redirect. Lyric didn't want to hurt his friend. He wanted to reach the people behind his friend.

Ren held the flank without being asked. Sera stood apart, watching, unwilling to fight on either side. Maren was in front of the tent with her arms spread, not because she could stop Lyric physically, but because she couldn't do nothing.

Mira watched from inside. She was holding Senna's hand.

The fight lasted minutes. It felt like hours. Kael blocked every path, absorbed every strike, channeled every Echo he had into the simple act of standing between his best friend and three people who trusted him.

Meanwhile, outside the observatory, the tears kept spreading.

Lyric fell to one knee. Beaten. Not defeated — the difference between a body that couldn't continue and a mind that still believed it should.

"You win," he said. "For now."

---

The ground tore open.

Not a normal tear — a rupture. The largest Veil breach the group had ever seen split the earth like a wound, and through it came something massive. The **Shattered Colossus** — a titan of corrupted stone and dark energy — dragged itself into the world. The ground shook. Crystal formations shattered. The Echoes in the area scattered.

The eruption was accelerating. The Colossus was a symptom, not a cause — the Veil was breaking down faster, and larger things were coming through. If this was happening here, it was happening elsewhere too.

Kael and Lyric looked at each other across the broken ground.

No words. No agreement. Just the muscle memory of a thousand fights together.

They moved as one.

The Colossus fought in phases. First a healing crystal behind its body, regenerating its damage — Sera's magic smashed it while Ren absorbed the boss's ground slams. Then the Colossus charged a devastating attack that required focused damage to interrupt — Kael called the timing, Lyric redirected the team's output. Then the battlefield split, a rift of energy dividing the ground into two halves, forcing the group to fight on both sides simultaneously.

Kael and Lyric's coordination was flawless. They didn't speak. They didn't need to. Even now, even with everything broken between them, the fighting was perfect.

The Colossus fell.

---

After.

The group stood in the ruins of the observatory courtyard. Everyone was injured. The Veil around them was seething — tears opening and closing rapidly, the ripping sounds coming from every direction now. The eruption hadn't slowed. If anything, the Colossus fight had made it worse — all that channeled energy, all that Veil interaction, feeding back into the instability.

They could feel it: the Veil was on the verge of something catastrophic. Not just here. The tears had been spreading outward for days. Whatever was happening in the Shattered Lands was rippling across the continent.

Lyric and Kael stood facing each other. The fight was over. The boss was dead. Nothing was resolved. And the world was running out of time.

Lyric looked at Senna. At Mira, holding Senna's hand, staring at the ground. At Otho, clutching his notebook. At Maren, who was crying and trying not to. At Sera, who was very still. At Ren, who was watching Kael and waiting.

Then back at Kael.

"I still think I'm right," Lyric said.

"I know."

"But I'm going to do something stupid instead. Because you're my friend, and I want to believe you're not wrong."

He didn't explain further. He walked past Kael, past the group, toward the largest active rupture in the Veil — the gash the Colossus had torn through, three meters wide and growing, light and corruption pouring through it.

And he channeled. Not through Echoes. Not through a Veilborn intermediary. Through himself. Directly into the tear.

A Resonant's attunement wasn't meant to interact with the Veil directly — Resonant energy could amplify Echoes, but it couldn't reshape the Veil. That was a Veilborn's domain. What Lyric did wasn't reshaping. It was cruder. He jammed raw energy into the rupture like plugging a hole with his fist — not sealing it, not closing it properly, just filling the gap with himself until the edges held.

It wouldn't last. The Veil would eventually push his energy out, like a body rejecting a foreign object. But it would hold for now. The tear narrowed. The smaller tears around it snapped closed. The ripping sounds stopped — not all of them, not the distant ones, but the local eruption point sealed. The sky above the observatory went solid for the first time since they'd entered the Shattered Lands.

It wasn't a permanent fix. The Veil was still thin. The Sovereign was still advancing. The tears would start again — in days, maybe weeks. But the eruption point was cauterized. The bleeding had slowed.

"Find another way," he said. His voice was barely audible over the energy coursing through him. "You owe me that."

Light poured from his eyes and his hands. Then it stopped.

He collapsed. The sky held.

Senna knelt beside him and closed his eyes. Mira was crying silently, her face pressed into Senna's shoulder. Otho turned away. Maren made a sound that wasn't quite a word.

Kael stood very still for a very long time.

---

In the days that followed, news trickled in from the south. The eruption's ripple effect had reached settlements hundreds of miles away — tears opening in regions that hadn't seen Voidspawn activity in years. Settlements that had been safe behind defensive lines were suddenly exposed. Some held. Some didn't.

Kael didn't ask for details. He didn't ask which settlements had fallen, or how many people had died, or whether any of them were places the group had helped in earlier regions. He didn't ask because he already knew the answer: some of them were. They had to be. The eruption had been spreading for days while the group deliberated, fought each other, and fought the Colossus. Every hour of delay had been an hour the tears expanded.

Whether acting sooner would have saved those settlements — whether anything could have saved them — was a question that had no answer. But the question existed, and it sat in the space between Kael and everyone who looked at him.

---

The group fractured.

Not in a dramatic confrontation — in quiet conversations over the next day, as people made their choices.

Ren stayed without hesitating. When one of the departing fighters stopped to ask him directly, "You're staying?" Ren didn't look up from checking his equipment. "I'm with Kael." The person waited for more. There wasn't more. Ren had made his choice years ago, and everything about him — his positioning, his silence, his readiness — reflected that clarity. He checked his weapons methodically and waited for Kael to tell him what to do next.

Sera packed methodically. "I can't stay and watch you let this happen," she said to Kael. Not cruelly — with the blunt certainty of someone who respected him but disagreed. "I'm going to do something useful. Move Veilborn away from population centers. It's practical. It's something." She headed east.

Maren was the one that hurt.

She loved Senna. She'd been the group's healer, its heart alongside Lyric. But she could see the math. She'd watched soldiers die at Hallen because of a compromise, and now settlements had fallen while the group was fighting itself instead of fighting the problem.

Before she left, she went to where Lyric's body lay, prepared for the burial rites that wouldn't come. She touched his hand — once, briefly. "You deserved better than being right," she whispered. Not to him, since he was already gone, but into the space his absence had left. Then she stood and walked away from the camp, tears on her face, leaving behind Senna and the cause she'd believed in.

Senna watched her go. She didn't respond. She didn't move. She stood in the observatory doorway with Mira's hand in hers and watched until Maren was out of sight.

---

Kael took Lyric's training tag from his friend's body. It was worn smooth from years around his neck — a simple metal disc with his name stamped into it.

He put it around his own neck. He didn't explain.

Five people remained: Kael, Ren, Senna, Mira, Otho. Two fighters. Three non-combatants.

The warmth of the Frontier, the Barracks, the Synergy Trials — all of it was gone. What remained was grief and a promise.

*Find another way.*
